Ask a Mentor

A space to bring real questions, explore options, and grow as a leader—one honest conversation at a time.

The Ask a Mentor space is designed for leaders and emerging leaders who want a sounding board. It’s a place to bring real situations, “I’m not sure what to do here” moments, and questions about leading with both grace and truth. You don’t have to have it all figured out—you just have to be willing to learn.

Overview

Use this page to submit questions about communication, team dynamics, feedback, difficult conversations, culture, or your own leadership growth. Mentors will respond with practical, values-centered guidance that you can apply in everyday situations.

This space is meant to be:

  • Supportive — focused on encouragement, growth, and learning.
  • Practical — offering real-world options, not just theory.
  • Respectful — protecting the dignity of you and those you lead.
  • Confidential in spirit — no names, no identifying details, just the heart of the situation.

When you submit a question, avoid using real names or confidential information. Share enough context for mentors to understand the situation, and we’ll help you think through next steps.

Recent Questions & Mentor Responses

Below are sample questions and responses to show how this space can be used. In a live forum, new questions would appear here once a mentor has replied.

How do I address a pattern of lateness without damaging trust?

Topic: Accountability Role: New Leader Answered

I have a team member who regularly shows up late to meetings and occasionally to their shift. They’re strong in their role and well-liked by the team, but this is starting to affect others. I don’t want to overreact, but I also don’t want to ignore it. How do I address this in a way that’s firm but still respectful?

Mentor Response

Start with a private, calm conversation that names the pattern, not just a single incident. Lead with grace: acknowledge their strengths and contributions first. Then share what you’re noticing: “I’ve noticed a pattern of arriving late to meetings, and it’s starting to impact the team.”

Ask an open question: “Is there something getting in the way of being on time?” Listen fully before responding. Then bring in truth by clarifying expectations and impact. Work together on one or two specific next steps, and schedule a follow-up. Grace opens the door; truth keeps the standard clear.

How can I give feedback to my own manager?

Topic: Upward Feedback Role: Individual Contributor Answered

My manager often cancels our 1:1s at the last minute. I know they’re busy, but it leaves me unsure how I’m doing and what to prioritize. I’d like to share how this is affecting me without sounding ungrateful or demanding. Any suggestions?

Mentor Response

When giving feedback upward, focus on impact and partnership. You might say, “I know you have a lot on your plate, and I appreciate the time you invest in our team. When our 1:1s get moved or canceled, I sometimes feel unclear about priorities and next steps. Could we find a way to protect even a shorter, consistent check-in?”

This approach affirms their reality, names your experience, and offers a solution-oriented request. Grace shows up in your tone and assumptions; truth shows up in your clarity and courage.

What do I do when I feel like I’m not ready to lead?

Topic: Confidence Role: New Leader New

I was recently promoted into a leadership role, and some days I feel like I’m faking it. I’m worried people will notice I don’t have everything figured out. How do I build confidence without pretending to be someone I’m not?

Mentor Response

A mentor response is on the way. In the meantime, remember: feeling stretched is normal in new roles. Confidence often grows after we take action, not before.
